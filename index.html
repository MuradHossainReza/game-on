Swiss venison:
an extraordinary delicacy

Whether stag, boar or roe deer - it' s getting wild at Farmy! In autumn, Swiss hunters roam the forests in search of game meat. The fine-grained meat of stag, roe deer and wild boar is low in fat, contains hardly any cholesterol and has a high protein and mineral content. Combined with other fresh ingredients, it is easy to conjure up aromatic dishes with game. Game meat, venison or simply game, comes from wild animals that feed on their own in the wild and may be hunted according to Swiss hunting law. The hunter distinguishes between fallow deer (stags and roe deer), red deer (red deer and hinds) and wild boar.

Which wines
harmonise with game?

A dish and the corresponding wine should have the same style level. Exactly which specific drop depends on the type of game and its preparation. For example, the fruity aroma of a Pinot Noir or a soft Merlot blend goes wonderfully with gently cooked or pink-roasted venison and deer loin. For a hearty game goulash or other braised game dishes, spicy, warm red wines such as Monastrell from the Jumilla region and Malbec from southern France are excellent. Their dense berry and chocolate aromas are an exciting complement to the succinct aromas of the food.

Preparing game correctly

By preparing game properly, you ensure that it is well digestible and focus on the typical aroma of the valuable meat.

Paring :

Whether it's an aromatic saddle of stag or a delicious shoulder of deer - before you put the venison in the roaster, the pan or on the grill, you should remove fat, skins and tendons with a sharp knife. Otherwise the venison will bulge and become tough when roasted. Our tip: Since wild boar meat is somewhat fattier, you can cut off a little more of it when paring it. This makes the meat easier to digest after preparation and reduces the calories.

Pickling :

In the past, game meat often had an unpleasantly intense flavour that chefs tried to mask by soaking it in buttermilk for hours. This could occur if the game meat was not processed directly by the hunter after the hunt. Thanks to high food hygiene standards, such measures are no longer necessary today. Immediately after the hunt, the animals are broken open, cleaned, cut up and cooled by the hunter. This prevents bacteria from spreading and negatively affecting the taste.

Seasoning :

Too much salt and pepper will overpower the fine flavour of the game. Instead, enhance the natural flavour of the venison by using fresh spices and herbs such as bay leaf, marjoram, rosemary, thyme and juniper.

Very important: Despite all the precautions you take when hunting, you can never completely rule out the possibility of pathogens in the game. Therefore, always heat roe deer, venison and wild boar to 80 Â°C for at least 10 minutes before eating. It is best to use a meat thermometer.

What makes
the meat so healthy?

Because of its composition, venison from local forests is healthier than meat from conventional animal husbandry. This is mainly due to the long distances that the game- in contrast to its conspecifics from stables or enclosures - covers over hill and dale in their daily search for food. The movement in nature ensures that the animals hardly put on any fat in the summer months. Their meat is therefore rich in healthy unsaturated fatty acids and contains only small amounts of harmful cholesterol.

The diet of the animals has a great influence on the quality of the meat and contributes to the formation of the typical game flavour that is so popular with gourmets. An adult roe deer, for example, consumes two to four kilograms of greenery per day. Especially leaves, grasses and buds, but also red and black berries, beechnuts, acorns, herbs, mushrooms and moss are on the varied menu of the shy animals. The protein and valuable minerals from the food are stored in the meat and later benefit the connoisseur of the venison.

Why is game meat dark?

Game meat from roe deer, stag or wild boar is strikingly red in colour and considerably darker than other types of meat found in a butcher's display. There is a simple explanation as to why this is: Wild animals are on the move all day, which puts more strain on their muscles and intensifies their blood supply than is the case, for example, with animals in fenced enclosures. The blood pigments are deposited in the meat and give it its intense red colour. Another reason lies in the hunting itself. In contrast to conventional animal husbandry, game is shot by the hunter and not slaughtered. It therefore bleeds less, which is why the meat retains its dark colour even after cutting.
